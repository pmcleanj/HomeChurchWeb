May 2, 2004

Acts 9:36-43
Psalm 23
Revelation 7:9-17
John 10:22-30

I’d know that voice anywhere!

We all know people who seem to have naturally strong, distinct voices—voices we recognize instantly upon hearing them. Consider the following lines, and note how each speaker’s voice has become inextricably linked with the line itself:

“I’ll be back!” (Arnold Schwarznegger)
“I have a dream.” (Martin Luther King, Jr.)
“Go ahead, make my day.” (Clint Eastwood)

I’m sure each of us can come up with several examples of people in our own lives who have distinct, instantly recognizable voices. These voices can also run the gamut of responses that they inspire within us upon our hearing them. Some may grate on our nerves, and cause us to roll our eyes. Some may inspire us and call us to action. Some may soothe us and bring us comfort. Some may anger and frustrate us. Some may bore and exhaust us. As easily as we seem to recognize these voices, do we really know them?

I believe such knowing comes about only in the context of relationships. In our close relationships, the more intimately we get to know someone, the more adept we become at learning to recognize that person’s voice. Our ear becomes attuned to distinguishing the unique cadences, inflections, and variances in the voices of our closest loved ones.

Not only might we become skilled at singling out their voice amidst a cacophony of others; over time, we also might grow to anticipate the very words that our loved one might say. Perhaps it is a sort of sixth sense that helps us to grasp the unique “lingo” of our loved one, as we have moments in which we find ourselves able to finish that person’s sentence or share unspoken thoughts almost effortlessly. Not only have we come to know his or her audible, physical voice, but also the less tangible, all the more unique, expressions of that voice.

Link to the Gospel

This scene occurs in Jerusalem during the festival of Dedication--what most of us know, today, as Hanukah. The scene is a tense one, and entrapment seems to be the agenda at hand. The Jewish leaders encircle Jesus in the temple and confront him with a public question that is meant to challenge him. “How long will you keep us in suspense?” The Jews are anxious to clarify Jesus’ identity so they can identify and expel anyone who represents a threat to their establishment. The words they use to question Jesus are an idiomatic expression that might also be translated, “Why do you provoke us?” The challenge follows: “If you are the Messiah, tell us plainly.” They are seeking evidence that will help them to convict Jesus of blasphemy. They are agitated, hostile, and impatient.

The Jews may recognize Jesus’ voice, but they do not hear it or “know” it. They want a straightforward answer, directly from the mouth of Jesus. The irony is that, all along, the very words and actions of Jesus have served to testify to his Messiahship. The plain and simple truth is already before them; but, because they are not his sheep, they have not heard his voice. “The Father and I are one,” Jesus concludes. This oneness describes a close, interpersonal relationship--an intimate relationship that results in Jesus’ words and actions proclaiming the very message and will of God. Later in this gospel, in an extended prayer, Jesus invites his disciples into this same equation of oneness and solidarity (17:20-21). Just as Jesus and the Father are one, so does Jesus wish for his followers to be united as one community gathered around a shared promise. In turn we are called to proclaim the voice of the Messiah to the rest of the world in order to invite them in to that community.

Link to the First Reading

In this text we find more clues to understanding the significance of relationships in the kingdom of God. First, it is important to note that this is the only time the feminine form for “disciple” is used in the New Testament. Also, the wording makes it clear that Tabitha is not the only female disciple. This text provides a small bit of evidence to the fact that women also played a significant role in the early Christian community--ministry and missionary work was not solely reserved for men.

Miracles serve as a powerful tool for proclamation. Here in this text we see that Tabitha was a significant member of her community, both socially and in her service of mission. She was devoted to good works and acts of charity. Her death was likely to have direct ramifications on the community of widows gathered around her whom she helped to support.

It is not Peter who heals Tabitha in this story. Tabitha’s healing is carried out by the Lord through the power of relationships: Peter’s relationship to God in prayer; Peter’s relationship to the disciple, Tabitha; Tabitha’s relationship to her community. In this story the miracle is not the fact that Tabitha is brought back to life. The miracle occurs when “This became known throughout Joppa, and many believed in the Lord.” The Christian community continues to expand through the proclamation of God’s promises. A miracle points to something greater than itself, and it is the proclamation of that miracle that has a missionary effect. In our relationships we are invited to point to something greater than ourselves, to proclaim the promise of that voice that we have grown familiar with in our own lives.

Link to the Second Reading

In this apocalyptic text, the vision of community expands to its broadest to encompass, “a multitude ... from every nation, from all tribe and peoples and languages.” The scene is a victorious one--crowds robed in white with palm branches--but none have arrived at the scene without having first gone through the great ordeal. The community gathered is diverse and non-segregated. All are cloaked in the same white robe. The text emphasizes God’s sustaining promise to support the mission of their faithful witness. There will be suffering, pain, and sacrifice along the journey, but ultimately an idyllic scene reminiscent of Psalm 23 awaits those who persevere. Those who continue to listen to and follow the voice of their shepherding Lord and identify with his message and witness will be lead through suffering and death to the waters of eternal life.

QUESTIONS FOR DISCUSSION

  1. Who are some examples of people in your life whose voices are unique and distinct—you’d know them anywhere! Do you think you really “know” their voices, or just “recognize” them?

  2. Think for a moment about the voice of the good shepherd. Can you think of any ways in which you hear and experience this voice in your own life? Or can you think of examples of other people who might exemplify this shepherding voice to you? What thoughts come to mind?

  3. What do you think distinguishes miracles from good luck or good fortune? Have you ever experienced anything that might be considered a miracle? Do you think miracles only happen within the religious realm?

  4. What are your thoughts about the role of community and relationships in the Christian life? Do you think it is of primary importance? Does one have to attend church in order to be considered a Christian?
