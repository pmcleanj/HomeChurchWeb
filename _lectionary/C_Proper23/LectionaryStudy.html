Jeremiah 29:1, 4-7

An underlying theme of the Law ...

is the special protection the Law affords to strangers. That in itself is an interesting consideration in a time of xenophobia, when refugees and immigrants are being treated to hostility and distrust. But in this passage from Jeremiah, we see God's instruction on how to *be* a stranger. As usual it is not a plain reciprocity, God's people are held to God's standard of justice as much when they are strangers as when they are the land-holders.

Christians, being "dead in Christ and raised with Christ", are called to live in the world as strangers. The last thing that means is that we set up a "Christian Nation", ramming our cultic practices down the throat of our host culture and making ourselves obnoxious in the world. Nor are we to hold ourselves apart from our neighbours by being "of the world but not in it", as happens in some church communities where church-related events from financial counselling and basketball to bible-study and worship band practice demand participation every day of the week.

What are we to do, in the lands where we live as strangers? "Build houses and live in them; plant gardens and eat what they produce" -- live normal peaceful lives enjoying God's bounty, which God gives as generously to all people, not to Christians only. But more, we are to "seek the welfare of the city where I have sent you into exile ? for in its welfare you will find your welfare". This is a call to live in community transcends the lines of faith
