May 30, 2004
The Day of Pentecost

Acts 2:1-21
Psalm 104:25-35,37
Romans 8:14-17
John 14:8-27


A friend told of a Pentecost story that happened in her home church. Everything was ready for the day. People were dressed in red; red chrysanthemums gave fire to every corner of the sanctuary. Glorious hymns had been sung: “O Day Full of Grace,” “Veni Sanctus Spiritus.” The time came for the reading of the Pentecost text. One parishioner after another stood up to tell, in one language after another, of “the rush of a violent wind” that “filled the entire house where they were sitting,” of “divided tongues of as of fire” that descended upon the believers at the birth of the church.

Then a word came from the back and center of the sanctuary. “Fire!” No one moved, initially. None of the readers halted their speech. This must be one more way to bring the text of the day to life, one more way to envision the power unleashed through the gift of the Spirit. But the cry came louder, and with greater intensity. “Fire!” Now, all activity ceased. The worshipers, as one body, turned to see who it was that was making the cry, and why.

The words had been shouted by a well-respected member of the congregation. He held a fire-extinguisher in his left hand. The fire-extinguisher was dripping. “There’s a fire in the basement kitchen—we’ve got it somewhat under control, but we haven’t been able to get it out yet- it’s terribly smoky down there and is only going to get worse. I didn’t want the smoke or the smell to startle you in the middle of worship.” He laughed, “So I guess I interrupted everything instead.”

The fire was put out, and the fire damage tended to. The congregation remembers that Pentecost Sunday the day tongues of fire surely did move through their midst! Fire comes on Pentecost Sunday, fire and wind. We rejoice that the Spirit moves among us still, calling, gathering, enlightening, even as the Spirit moved over those first believers, creating, from them, the church. We celebrate the power of the Spirit among us— power as fire, as wind. It is the Spirit who gives life to our faith. It is through the Spirit that we are given precious gifts to share with the community—love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness. It is by the Spirit’s presence among us that we sing our witness to the one who was crucified but is now risen from the dead, by the Spirit’s presence that we are bold to share the gospel as the good news that it is.

Link to the Gospel

Philip wants one more sign that Jesus is of God. “Show us the Father,” he says, “and we will be satisfied.” One can almost imagine Jesus’ chagrin. “Have I been with you all this time, Philip, and you still do not know me?” Philip should see by now: Jesus and the Father are one. But Philip can’t see. His eyes are blinded to it. Indeed, it will take more than human understanding for any of the disciples to come to faith. So Jesus promises the gift of the other Advocate, the “Spirit of truth.” By the gift of the Spirit, the disciples’ eyes will be opened. They will be enabled to confess that is, as he has said, one with the Father.

Others will be as blind to the Spirit’s presence as was Philip to Jesus’ identity. The world “neither sees him or knows him.” The disciples will know of the presence of that fire, that wind, however. They will know because the Spirit will be present among them, making itself known. They will know the Spirit because the Spirit will “abide” with them, even “be in” them. They will know because the Spirit will move in their midst, reminding them of all that Jesus has said. Their belief itself will witness to its presence. And those in the world? They will need to see Jesus to see the Spirit, and need the Spirit to see Jesus.

QUESTIONS FOR DISCUSSION

  1. What does Jesus mean when he says that he is in the Father, and the Father in him?
  2. In what way does the Spirit keep us from being “orphaned?”

  3. In what way is the peace of which Jesus speaks connected to the gift of the Spirit?

Link to the Second Reading

Once more the note is sounded. The very ability to cry, “Abba, Father” is witness to the Spirit’s presence in the church. And the ability to cry, “Abba, Father” leads the church in the direction it takes toward life. We do not live by “fear”—anxious worry over what will happen in the next moment to us, to our loved ones, to the world. The ability to cry, “Abba, Father” bears witness to the hope that we have, in Christ, of seeing ourselves as “children of God.” It bears witness to the hope that we have, in Christ, of counted ourselves as having been adopted. And as beloved children, as loved ones taken into a family, we know we are, and will be, well taken care of. We will be “glorified” with him.

QUESTIONS FOR DISCUSSION

  1. What baptismal motifs do you see in this passage?
  2. What is it to be in “slavery to fear?”
  3. Why is suffering with Jesus come hand-in-hand of glorification with him?

Link to the First Reading

This is the story of the birth of the church. By the Spirit’s mighty presence, the faithful are given ability to witness to Christ in one language after another, for the sake of one people after another. The gift of the Spirit is never an end in itself. The Spirit is given for the sake of witness, for the sake of speaking the Word to “every nation.”

QUESTIONS FOR DISCUSSION

  1. This passage describes the Spirit’s work in the early church. How does the Spirit work in the church today? When and where do we find evidence of its presence?

  2. The gift of the Spirit comes as a mighty wind, as tongues of fire. Why are these appropriate images for the Spirit’s presence?
