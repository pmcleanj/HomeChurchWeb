Sermon Seeds

Focus Scripture:
Acts 7:55-60

Weekly Theme:
Faith's Foundation

Focus Statement:
“He gazed into heaven and saw the glory of God and Jesus standing at the right hand of God.”

Questions:
We all share the desire to find our way home, whether it’s at the end of a long day or at the end of a long journey, a long time of wandering, of alienation, of homesickness and pain. That may be why so many people speak of finding a church “home” when they find a congregation that welcomes them and feels like a place in which they can grow their faith. Of course, if a church only makes us comfortable with our lives as they are, our presuppositions and our compromises in faith, then perhaps we are speaking of accommodation rather than challenge and growth. Nevertheless, for many people, finding a church home means finding a safe haven, a refuge, a fortress, and a rock. The church may be the one way that they experience God’s protective love in a hostile and dangerous world.

It’s in that safe haven that we receive, like little babies, the spiritual milk that we need to grow our faith. We ourselves become part of the very “structure,” the home, the spiritual house that First Peter speaks of. If we remember that his audience was a group of dispossessed people, people who had no unifying dignity and identity apart from being a church, the power of these words expands in our hearing. Can you imagine being “no people” and then becoming “God’s people”? Have you ever felt like a “nobody” and then a “somebody”? Have you ever felt outside and alone, and then part of something greater than yourself? What would it sound like to your ears, if you thought you were “nobody,” and then became part of a “chosen race,” and words like “holy” and “royal” were used to describe you? What would it feel like to come out of darkness into the “marvelous light” of God?

The Acts reading recounts the violent reaction to Stephen’s sermon. Stephen was supposed to be serving tables (the hospitality committee?) but he got it into his heart and mind and spirit that he needed to climb into the pulpit and preach. When he re-told the story of his people and reminded them of their tendency to ignore the prophets, including Jesus, they turned on him and killed him, while Saul, the future martyr and apostle, watched their cloaks. This short passage reminds us of various moments in the life of Jesus, from the Transfiguration (he must have looked radiant when he saw the heavens open) to the Baptism of Jesus (again, the skies opening), the surrender of his spirit and the forgiveness for those who did not know what they were doing.

Somehow, Stephen’s sermon did not go over as well as Peter’s had, on Pentecost. Our witness is better received in some times and places than in others, and falls on more hospitable ears. We may have no way of knowing the results of what we do and say, and some days are better than others in the life of the church.

Stephen’s sermon spoke of the foundations of faith that had been laid down long ago, strong foundations, deep roots, powerful witnesses and lives to remember. The people’s shared experience, their walk, with God, including the times they failed to listen to and heed the still-speaking God, provided stones sunk deep into their collective religious memory. First Peter, though, calls the people “living stones” and refers to a precious cornerstone, again, addressing those who must have felt that they had been nobodies in a hostile world and needed a safe and welcoming place, a home. In the Gospel of John, Jesus uses the language of place, too, when he promises to prepare a dwelling place for his followers, who were bound to encounter the opposition and loneliness experienced by the dispossessed in First Peter and the martyrs in the Book of Acts.

And so, today, people of all kinds find their way to our churches. How is your church a safe haven, a refuge, a place of dignity and identity for those who seek a church home? What beliefs, stories, sufferings, and joys are sunk deep into the foundations of your church’s shared experience? In what ways does the still-speaking God call your church to become more welcoming and more energetic in conveying and embodying that welcome? We have it backwards if our desire to welcome people is in order to increase our membership: welcoming is who we are and have always been: “Because of its peculiar faith and its identity formed in hurt, the church is a unified community capable of a risking hospitality.” But it’s not our church, or our home, that we open to others: “The householder, God, has sole authority over admission at the doorway” (Brueggemann et al, Texts for Preaching, Year A).

How deep is our conviction that we somehow “own” the church, that it “belongs” to us in some way? Would it offend or change or affect us and our behavior if we truly embraced the belief that God is the householder in this “church home,” not us? What is “the hurt” that formed our identity?

Another thing to consider is our identity in a world that often blurs into our life – the world of materialism and success and growth and prosperity is awfully easy to emulate in the life of the church. Do we try to imitate the methods and tactics of the world around us when we take on the challenges of church life, or do we seek other sources of wisdom and strength? What are the boundaries that define us? How does the word “boundary” strike us when we talk about the church?

“The Christian community in North America has so bought into the world’s economics, its psychology, its standards of morality that visitors from outer space would have a difficult time discerning the difference between the social and political culture of the day, with its civil religion, and the church.” (Brueggemann et al, Texts for Preaching, Year A)

What makes us a distinctive people? Could a visitor tell the difference between your church and the surrounding culture? In what ways? In what ways might the surrounding culture have valuable gifts and support for our life in the church? In what ways do we – must we – stand apart and declare ourselves different? When have you experienced the church as “the headlights” and when has it been “the tail lights” in the journey toward justice and righteousness? In other words, when has the surrounding culture led the way and the church, ironically, had to catch up? (One might think of the ordination of women.) How has the still-speaking God, at times, spoken to the church through outsiders? (Think of the ancient example of Ruth, the Moabite widow, speaking to Naomi.)

How do you and your congregation experience the still-speaking God today as a refuge, a rock, a fortress? What is the call that, like Stephen, you hear that makes you preach? Is it ironic that, in these readings, “stones” can build but they can also kill? Who really lived and who really died in the story of Stephen? Where is life, and where is death?

One commentary (The Cultural World of Jesus, by John Pilch) turns to John Dominic Crossan’s translation of the familiar “I am the way…” verse from John: “I am the authentic (truth) vision (way) of existence (life).” Pilch says: “Jesus’ words and deeds in this Gospel speak love at every turn. He demonstrates absolute, total, and universal love in his varied responses to those who approach him. Jesus’ life, teaching, and behavior do indeed present people with ‘an authentic vision of human existence,’ that is, a model of the way human life ought to be lived. If one lives like this, one will definitely encounter God, who is Love.” This was a reassuring message to the early Jewish Christians, who could have experienced great anxiety over losing their spiritual home (the synagogue) because of their belief in Jesus. How is it a consoling message to members of your church, and the wider church, today?

Fifth Sunday of Easter
April 24, 2005
Acts 7:55-60

DOSHISHA

With its 1,400 Buddhist temples and 400 Shinto shrines, the ancient city of Kyoto is a tranquil oasis in the midst of the noise and tumult of modern Japan. The tree-shaded campus of Doshisha University in Kyoto, too, provides quiet corners for meditation and reflection. But Doshisha, which was founded by Christians more than a century ago, has not turned its back on the contemporary world. Its curriculum seeks to instill both the knowledge and the moral values necessary to the challenges of the 21st century.

Martha Mensendiek, a United Church of Christ missionary who teaches Social Work at Doshisha, embraces that vision not only in the classroom, but also as advisor to the Habitat for Humanity chapter on campus. A few years ago, she led eight of her students on the chapter’s first project: to build a duplex in Bacolod, in the Negros Occidental region of the Philippines.

Even as she shared in their hard work of mixing cement by shovel, Martha watched her students change right before her eyes. Most had never worked in construction, let alone visited a developing country where poverty and misery are commonplace. “But that did not stop them from enjoying themselves,” Martha recalls. “Working together with the Filipinos we sang songs and had fun communicating across language barriers as we worked.”

Their Filipino hosts’ broad smiles and deep Christian faith proved infectious. Each day began with a period of devotions. Although none of the Japanese students was a Christian, all joined in singing the hymns their Filipino friends taught them. “It was wonderful to witness the Spirit at work bringing students in touch with the joy of Christian community,” Martha Mensendiek says.

“This trip opened many doors for many people,” she continues. “It opened the physical ‘door’ for two families who moved out of the slums into a decent house. It opened many hearts to friendships across boundaries and to the joy of volunteer work and community building.” And it opened the door to many more mission trips with many more students. “I feel truly blessed to be given a part in this kind of ministry,”Martha says.

All of us in the United Church of Christ are blessed by international partnerships such as we have through Martha Mensendiek with Doshisha University.

Liturgical notes on the Readings

In ecumenical liturgical practice, there are normally three readings and one psalm at each Sunday service, in this order:

First Reading: Hebrew Scripture
Response: Psalm (or Canticle) from the Bible
Second Reading: Epistle (or Acts or Revelation)
Third Reading: Gospel

The first two lessons are normally read by laypeople, the Gospel by a Minister of the Word or a layperson. In Roman Catholic, Anglican and liturgical Protestant churches, it is uncommon for an ordained minister to read all of the lessons.

The psalm is not a reading but a congregational response following the lesson from Hebrew Scripture: it is normally sung with a refrain or recited by the congregation as poetry. Occasionally, a canticle is appointed in place of a psalm; it is sung or recited in the same way. The New Century Hymnal provides a complete liturgical psalter with refrains and music.

A hymn may be sung as an introduction to the proclamation of the Gospel.

During Ordinary Time (seasons after Epiphany and Pentecost) two alternative sets of OT readings with responsorial psalms are provided. The first option is a semi-continuous reading through a book of Hebrew Scripture; the second is thematically related to the other readings.
