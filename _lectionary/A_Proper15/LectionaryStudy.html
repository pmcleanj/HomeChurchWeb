Bible Passage

Genesis 45:1-15 and Psalm 133 or Isaiah 56:1, 6-8 and Psalm 67
Romans 11:1-2a, 29-32

This passage reminds us all of the grace of God. While we will continually debate what defiles and what doesn't defile and struggle with who is in and who is out, Paul reminds us of the promise of God. "For God has imprisoned all in disobedience so that he may be merciful to all" (Rom. 11:32). God continues to seek his divine redemption even in the midst of human mismanagement. Again we see that even our own disobedience cannot separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus. God remains steadfast and faithful to God's creation.
Matthew 15: (10-20), 21-28

Obviously, Jesus is not talking about media defiling the world. But he does make a statement, "it is not what goes into the mouth that defiles a person, but it is what comes out of the mouth that defiles." There is a concern in the passage for what is holy as opposed to what defiles. Jesus is talking in Matthew's Gospel specifically about ritual hand washing matters that separated the Pharisees' with the Matthean church. Boring in the New Interpreter's Bible makes the statement, "Christians of every generation need to see themselves as belonging to a community called to holiness, and to ask what in our own time violates this holiness and nullifies the church's witness." Ultimately, Jesus says, it is not what goes in, but what comes out that defiles. It is interesting that off of this story of defilement comes the story of the Syrophoenicain Woman. These two stories set out to challenge what is clean and unclean, who is deserving and undeserving. I remember when I traveled to India, learning that it is considered "unclean" to offer one's left hand to another. Part of this has to do with health issues and cleanliness issues. In the United States offering one's left hand is not considered unclean. Questioning whether the media can defile and who the outsiders are in our society may be modern day equivalents to the questions raised in our Gospel

Pew-work Hand-outs

“Pew-work” is like Home-work, except that it is done in the pews, instead of being done at home. Because it is focussed on the readings (as the sermon, presumably, also is) it can be done during the sermon to help the listener concentrate. Or, it can be done while waiting for everyone else to finish their communion. It isn't done during prayers, or hymns, or the readings, because

  • During Prayers, we pray

  • During Hymns, we sing

During the Readings, we listen
