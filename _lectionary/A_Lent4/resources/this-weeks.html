The Opening of Eyes

A few weeks ago I attended a Gonzaga Men’s Basketball Game.  Although all of the seats in the arena are pretty good, the particular seats that my husband and I were sitting in were close to the top.  As we sat there, they announced that it was time for “fan of the game”.  Because I did not really know the number of my seat nor did I expect to win, I paid no attention to the announcement blaring over the loudspeaker.  It was only when my husband nudged me and said, “That’s your seat.  You’ve won,” that I realized I was “Les Schwab Fan of the Game!”  If he hadn’t said anything, or if there was a way for someone else to have taken our seats DOWN IN THE VERY FRONT ROW, I would have lost without even knowing what I had gained.  I was chosen and yet I was blind until my eyes were opened!

Link to the First Reading

The epic story of King David begins with these verses that we have today.  God tells the prophet Samuel that Saul is out, a new king is in.  A king born in Bethlehem.  God provides the action to this story.  While Samuel is the reluctant messenger (he’s afraid he might be killed by King Saul’s people) he does not think up his own actions.  Everything from the denouncing of King Saul to his trip to Bethlehem to the stretching of the truth that he tells all come from God.

After Samuel has gathered with Jesse and his sons, Samuel begins to take back the reins.  Jesse’s first son, Eliab, apparently looked “kingly.”  Because of this, Samuel is ready to anoint him as king.  But God rejects him.  “Do not look on his appearance or on the height of his stature.”  The next son is also rejected.  And the next.  And the next.  All of the first seven sons of Jesse are “passed over.”

And then they wait for that last son.  They wait.  And they wait.  As Samuel says to Jesse, “We will not sit down until he comes here.”  And finally “the one” comes.  Interestingly, the first thing that is described is David’s appearance (after we heard that God only looks in the heart and not the physical attributes).  Apparently he is handsome, movie star gorgeous.  The writer cannot help but comment on his beauty.

Again in this story, the action does not come from the people.  David does not initiate a thing.  From how the story reads, we know nothing about David except that he is attractive.  When he shows up, passively he is anointed by Samuel.  There is no word from David.  He doesn’t even question his destiny.  God has chosen him.  He will be king.

Link to the Gospel

Keeping in mind that the gospel of John always has multi-layers of meaning, two of the major thrusts for John are the themes of light and darkness and seeing and not seeing.  Both of these themes are in today’s gospel reading.

There are many stories of people begging Jesus to heal them.  This is not one of those stories.  The blind man apparently is just there.  Jesus pulls him into his life in order to make a point with his disciples.  When Jesus is asked, “Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents, that he was born blind?” Jesus uses the blind man as a life-size example.

Again, as in the David story, this blind man is passive.  The action is done to him, not by him.  Jesus spits and makes mud, spreading the mud on this man’s eyes.  (Interestingly, the Greek word “epichriein” is used here – meaning “anointed” – as in Samuel anointing David for kingship.)  The blind man is commanded to go to a pool and wash and when he does this, his eyes are opened.

This man, chosen by Jesus, now sees.  He not only sees trees and birds and flowers, people and objects, but he sees much more than that as well.  At the end of the story, the now-seeing man declares his faith, “Lord, I believe.”

Meanwhile, the Pharisees are still blinded.  Throughout Jesus’ story in John, the people continue to be divided amongst the chosen and the not chosen, the seeing and the blind, the children of light and the children of darkness.

This blind man now can see because he is has been chosen as a child of the light.

Link to the Second Reading

Paul’s letter declares that those who follow Christ are the people of the light.  He says that if you have been chosen, if you have had your eyes opened, then no longer sit in the darkness.  The light has come – now live in the light.

It is true that even after one is chosen, it is easy to slip back into the ways of darkness.  The pull of the darkness is sometimes too hard to resist.  Looking at the Old Testament reading, the anointed King David slipped a time or two into that power of darkness.  For him, repentance came, but so did heartache and misery.  He was a child of the light and yet he “took part in the unfruitful works of darkness.”

Instead of this, Paul urges those in the light to stay in the light.  In 5:1 Paul states, “Be imitators of God, therefore, as dearly loved children.”  First, we are to remember that we are “dearly loved children.”  We are not outsiders, we are not blind, we are not in the blackness of the night – no!  We are “dearly loved children!”  And so, in response to this gift of light, we are to “imitate” God.  In other words, we are called to be as Jesus was.  We are called to love as Jesus loved, live as Jesus lived – for the “fruit of the light is found in all that is good and right and true.”

What a great gift to be chosen.  Let us live in this light!

Questions for Discussion
  1. In the Lutheran Church many churches now include in the Rite of Baptism a time when oil is placed upon the baptized person’s forehead.  It is reminiscent of Samuel anointing David when he became king.  How is baptism like David’s anointing?  How is it not?
  2. It is widely known that even though David was chosen for greatness, he did not always act in a great way.  How do you feel about his behavior and the forgiveness he received?   Is this comforting for us and our relationship with God?  Or is this aggravating? 
  3. In many Bible studies I have had, people become concerned with those who are not chosen.  Is this a concern of yours?  How is this addressed in these scriptures today?  In other scriptures throughout the Bible?
  4. Jesus’ healing stories always bring up questions about those not healed.  What about the hundreds of blind men in Palestine who did not receive their sight from Jesus?   Does this have bearing to you in this story?  In your life?
  5. How are we given the gift of sight like the blind man?  How are we like the Pharisees?
  6. In Ephesians, Paul urges us to be “imitators of God.”  What does this mean?  How is this a response to God and not a call to “work for salvation?”
