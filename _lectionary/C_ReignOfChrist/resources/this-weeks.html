Christ the King?

We all know what kings and queens are like.  Just ask any kid and you will get many of the same answers.  A king has lots of money and gold.  A king can tell anyone to do whatever he wants them to do.  A king wears a crown.  A king has a big kingdom and lots of soldiers.  And the number one answer is that a king always lives in a big castle.  We all know about what kings are supposed to look like.  Yet, on the last Sunday of the Church year we celebrate Christ our King.  Jesus looks like a very different kind of king that the picture that any kid will paint for you. 

Jesus hangs on a cross, bloody and defeated.  The only similarity he has to the description of what we all know a king is supposed to look like is that he has a crown on his head, but this crown is a crown of thorns not of gold and jewels.  A sign is above his head which says “The King of the Jews,” but all who see Jesus hanging on the cross know that this man is no king. 

Jesus is far from the kind of king, from the kind of Messiah that the Jewish people were expecting.  The Jews knew that there was a Messiah coming, one who would come to save them.  They knew that this Messiah would be one from the line of King David. He would be the one to come and free them from the oppression that they were experiencing under the Roman occupation of their promised land, Israel.  This King would rise up and be a REAL king who would come with power and might and save them.  This Jesus who was hanging on the cross was not the king that they had expected.  He was powerless, dying on a cross.  How could he save anyone, he could not even save himself? 

We often hear the same cries about God today.  Why did God not heal my mother?  How come God let so many hurricanes hit Florida, haven’t they had enough?  How come God doesn’t make us all get along so that there is no more war?  Today God seems to lack the power to save us, as well.  There is still sickness, poverty, and injustice.  It seems that evil still wins the day.  We want a God who will come with power to make the world right.  Just like the Jewish people of Jesus’ time wanted a Messiah to come in power and save them from Roman occupation.  We celebrate Christ the King this Sunday, yet Jesus looks far from regal.  We wonder, what kind of king is this Jesus anyway?


Link to the Gospel Reading

The soldiers, leaders, and even the criminal on the cross taunt Jesus.  They cry out to him, “He saved others, let him save himself if he is the Messiah of God, his chosen one,” or “If you are the king of the Jews, save yourself, “ or “Are you not the Messiah, save yourself and us.”  There is the cry again and again, that if Jesus is truly the Messiah, truly a king then he should show his power and save himself.  He should come down off the cross and prove he is the Messiah, the chosen one of God to all the world.  This is the kind of king that the people expect, a king who comes in power and might, a king who would show the world that he is truly the King of the Jews. 

In the movie and the book the Last Temptation of Christ, the last temptation is just this, to come down off the cross.  Jesus is tempted to save himself.  The movie then goes on to show what Jesus’ life would have been like if he had come down off the cross and saved himself.  Many years after Jesus came down from the cross this Jesus encounters Paul.  Paul is preaching on the streets, preaching Christ crucified, preaching about the death and resurrection of Jesus.  Jesus tries to confront Paul and tell him that no, he did not die on the cross.  Instead he came down off the cross.  Paul responds saying essentially, “No that kind of Messiah who comes down from the cross to save himself does not save us.  Instead the only hope is the resurrected Jesus.  The resurrected Jesus saves the world.”  Right there, that is the crux of it all.  The only way that we are saved is through Jesus death on a cross.  The cries of the people are to save himself to prove he is a king, and yet by staying on the cross Jesus is doing just that.  Just when Jesus looks least like a king through the world’s standards he is acting most kingly.  A king is not one who saves himself but saves others.  He cannot save himself if he is to save others.  So he dies on a cross that we can be saved.  He is a Messiah, a king, who saves others by not saving himself. 

The second criminal gets this and asks Jesus, “Remember me when you come into your kingdom.”  To which Jesus responds “Truly, I tell you, today you will be with me in Paradise.”  Jesus hanging there dying of a cross between two criminals and he is offering paradise, the kingdom of God, and forgiveness of sins to a criminal, one of the ones who is most in need.  Jesus truly is Christ our King.  It is in this picture of Jesus that we see what kind of a king we truly have.  We have a king who comes to save us and who gives his life and then rises again so that our sins are forgiven and we may be saved.

Link to the First Reading

The king of Judah during the time of the prophet Jeremiah was not following in the ways of the Lord.  He was the shepherd who led the flocks, the chosen people of God astray.  Because of his actions the people of God would be taken from their land into exile.  At the time of these verses from Jeremiah, the people are in despair.  They are being taken from their homes and shipped off to foreign lands.  They feel abandoned and deserted by God.  They are hopeless.  Jeremiah the prophet has a word from God for the people.  He tells them that all is not lost, God will act.  It is only through the Lord’s intervention that the people of God will be saved.  The Lord says, “I myself will gather the remnant of my flock out of all the lands where I have driven them, and I will bring them back to their fold, and they shall be fruitful and multiply (v. 3).”  

Eventually God does keep this promise and after 70 years in exile the people come back to the promised land.  But this is not all that God promises. The Lord says, “The days are surely coming when I will raise up for David a righteous Branch, and he shall reign as king and deal wisely, and shall execute justice and righteousness in the land (v. 5).”  It is because of these words and others like them that the Jewish people waited for the earthly king to come and rule again.  We see that this was fulfilled in Jesus, but in an unexpected way.  Instead of coming in power to rule, Jesus came to seek and to save the lost, to heal the sick and save the sinner.  Jesus came in weakness, suffered and died. But that was not the end of the story …

Link to the Second Reading

Jesus rose again.  In his resurrection he overcame all the powers of sin, death, and the devil.  Our lesson from Colossians shares with us that through the death and resurrection of Jesus “God has rescued us from the power of darkness and transferred us into the kingdom of his beloved Son, in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins (v.13-14).”  Jesus is truly the king and has brought us into his kingdom through the forgiveness of sins.  Just as Jesus forgave the thief on the cross and brought the thief into paradise with him, it is through Jesus’ forgiveness of our sins through his death on the cross that we are brought into paradise.  The king has come to rescue us and that rescue can only come, through staying on the cross and dying for us.  That it was through the blood of the cross that we have peace with God.  Colossians tells us that “through him God was pleased to reconcile to himself all things, whether on earth or in heaven, by making peace through the blood of his cross.”  Through the blood, the king dying of the cross, we are forgiven and at peace with the Lord.  Jesus truly is Christ our King.

Questions for Discussion

  1. How do you envision a king? 
  2. As we look at the world around us we see that the world is far from perfect.  How does Jesus death and resurrection speak to a world which is still in pain?  Is our God a powerless God?
  3. Many struggle with the Son of God dying of a cross.  It doesn’t make sense.  How do you share with those who struggle about why God would send his Son to die for us?
  4. In what ways has the suffering of God touched your life?  How would it be different if God had come in power instead of in weakness?
