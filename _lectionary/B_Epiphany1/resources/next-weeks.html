Mighty Waters, Mighter Words

I moved to the Midwest two and a half years ago after I graduated from Pacific Lutheran University to attend seminary.  When I tell people that I actually miss the rain, they look at me like I am crazy.  But I cannot help it.  The rain to me is a powerful symbol of life and renewal and I sometimes feel lost without it. Now, of course we do get our share of occasional thunderstorms and downpours.  But I am not talking about those torrential floods.  What I miss is that constant steady stream that drip-drops, pitter-patters on the windshield as you are driving; that runs down your umbrella in little rivulets and collects in various boot-stomping puddles; that soaks and seeps deep into the muddy earth quenching the thirst of all that is green and growing.  If anyone should understand what baptism is all about, it should be the people of the rain.  For the people of the rain are daily reminded of the waters that washed over them in their baptism to bring about cleansing and forgiveness of sins, and new life in Christ. 

But baptism is not all about the water.  For baptism is a sacrament, and a sacrament is a sign of God’s grace through an earthly element combined with God’s Word.  There is nothing mysterious or magical about the properties of water alone.  Broken down into its most basic form, it is simply two hydrogen atoms combined with one oxygen atom.  It is God’s Word that gives this simple substance the power to bring about new life and to make God’s love known to the Children of God.  It is God’s mighty Word that makes the Holy Spirit’s presence known to us in baptism.

Link to the First Reading

When does life begin?  Traditionally our interpretation of the first creation account in Genesis has lead us to understand that God created the world out of nothing – ex nihilo.  But more recent scholarship has challenged this traditional notion, noting from 1:2 that God is not working from a total blank slate, but begins with a few raw materials:  “The earth was a formless void and darkness covered the face of the deep.”  It was common in the Ancient World to believe that the earth originated from a deep watery abyss.  Water represented the uncontrollable chaos of the world, full of stormy waves and dangerous sea-monsters.  God’s act of creation was to step in and begin to form order out of chaos.  But whether or not God started from nothing or had something already there to work with, does not change the fact that Genesis marks this moment as God’s first act of creation, this moment as “The Beginning.”  Life begins at this moment.  Time begins at this moment.  Moreover, it begins through a Word from God.  God simply says, “Let there be light,” and there was light.  God brings order out of chaos through the simplicity of God’s spoken Word.   

When does life begin for Christians?  From the moment we are conceived we come equipped with all of the raw materials for human life – flesh, blood, breath, brains.  But in light of Genesis, is this the moment we should point to as our “beginning”?  Through baptism into the Holy Spirit, the water and the word combine to bring order and meaning where there once was only chaos and hopelessness.  Through baptism into the Holy Spirit we are reborn as Children of God and each day as we remember our baptism our life begins anew again.    

Link to the Second Reading

The disciples in Ephesus may have been disciples of John the Baptist.  They have received John’s baptism but this is not enough.  Paul explains that they must be baptized into the Holy Spirit, which they have never heard of until now.  John’s baptism of repentance was an important step, but, according to Paul, its real significance was to point to the one who was to come after him.   

Immediately upon hearing this, the disciples choose to be baptized in the name of the Lord and they receive the Holy Spirit through the laying on of hands.  The power of the Holy Spirit enables them to speak mighty words through the Spirit’s gifts of tongues and prophecy.  Through their baptism into the Holy Spirit, the power of God’s spoken Word works in them and through them continuing to communicate God’s grace to the community.

The Gospel Reading

John the Baptist must have been quite a figure.  He had achieved a certain amount of fame and people came from everywhere to see him.  They trekked all the way out into the wilderness to get a glimpse of this strange man who wore camel’s hair and ate locusts and wild honey.  They made pilgrimages to hear this man speak and to be invocated into his baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins.  But the words that they heard him speak were not what they might have expected.  For his words did not point to himself but to someone else.  Despite John the Baptist’s growing fame and rumored reputation, there was one about to come after him who would be even mightier.  “For I have baptized you with water; but he will baptize you with the Holy Spirit.” 

The introduction of Jesus into the gospel of Mark begins here.  There is no record or account of his life previous to this moment.  Whatever happened in his prior life is irrelevant to the story.  Mark’s account of Jesus begins with his baptism and it is quite an introduction.  Upon Jesus’ baptism, the heavens open up and the Spirit descends in the form of a dove.  We tend to read this as being a primarily graceful, serene moment.  However, it must have been anything but that.  For the word in Greek used to describe the heavens opening up (schizomenous) is where we get our word “schism” from.  It literally means to tear apart or to rip open violently and is actually a rather terrifying image.   

So, too, is it with the dove.  The dove is a symbol of peace and yet few people realize that it is one of the more ferocious birds that exist in the feathered kingdom.  But the dove is not a symbol of peace because of its gracefulness or docility as people tend to think. In the Ancient World, doves were used as carriers to deliver messages to far away peoples, much like pigeons have been used in the more recent past.  Thus, doves became a symbol of peace because they provided a means for communication, and peace comes through words and communion with others. 

Through baptism into the Holy Spirit, we receive God’s mighty Word into our own lives as God communicates God’s love to us.  Through baptism, we receive the peace to communicate that Word to others and to live out that Word in the world.

Questions for Discussion

1. Do you remember anything about your baptism?  If so, what do you remember?  If not, find someone who was present and ask him or her to tell you about it.  Does knowing the story of your baptism help you to know more about your identity as a child of God?

2. For myself, walking in the rain serves as a reminder of my baptism.  For others, making the sign of the cross on their forehead in the shower is their daily reminder.  What other ways can you think of to remember your baptism?  How might incorporating such a simple daily ritual of baptismal remembrance into your routine affect other areas of your life? 

3. What areas in your life do you feel that chaos has taken over?  What are the sea-monsters that you face?  How might God’s Word of grace help you find order and meaning again?

4. In their baptism into the Holy Spirit, the disciples at Ephesus were gifted with the ability to speak in tongues and to prophecy.  What gifts and abilities has the Spirit given to you?  How can you use these gifts to communicate God’s grace to others?
