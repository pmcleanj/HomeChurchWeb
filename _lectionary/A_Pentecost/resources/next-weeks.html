Something New is Blowing In

A pastor friend told me about a meeting he had with the church council where he was beginning interim ministry.  He said that every question he asked resulted in a lengthy recalling of their church's history. One council member introduced herself as new to the church.  When the pastor then asked how long she had been at this particular church she said, "Only 15 years."

One of the jobs of an interim pastor is to help a congregation  look towards the future.  As part of this process, the interim pastor asked the council: What are you looking forward to in the future?  Where do you want your church to be in five years?

The council was silent for quite a while. Finally the only forward-looking event that this council could come up with was the church’s 175th anniversary.  This would take place the following year.  Upon hearing this, the interim pastor knew that there was some work to be done.

Sadly, this church had no sense of its future.  It only saw itself in terms of its past.  And unless the people’s outlook changed, this church would not continue on into the future.  For as important as it is to celebrate past successes and anniversaries, it is essential that a church also look at what God is doing in the present and in the future.

In contrast to this congregation, the disciples stopped looking backward when they were filled with the Holy Spirit.

Link to the Gospel

As many of us have experienced, when someone dies the natural tendency is to look back upon a person’s life.  I am quite sure that when the disciples were gathered in that upper room, stories of Jesus would have come pouring out of each of the people.

“Remember when Jesus saved that wedding banquet.”  “Remember when that blind man could see?”  “Remember when Mary and Martha were so mad at Jesus for not being there for Lazarus?”  “Remember Lazarus coming out of the tomb?”  “Remember the soldiers?”  “Remember the cross?”

It is natural that as people struggle with their grief, stories come.  Memories flood your mind and your heart.  The sadness of the event and the overwhelming pain that is yours causes your mind to circle backward, toward happier times, when things were better.

But the disciples did not stay in that grief forever.  Really, it was a short-lived grief.  For on that first Easter evening suddenly Jesus stood among them.  And for Jesus, as much as he always loved a good story, he did not look backward.  Instead he first gave them comfort and healing.  “Peace be with you.”  Then he told them that they were to leave the relative safety of their shared story and tell the story to those who had not heard.  “As the Father has sent me, so I send you.” (20:21)

The disciples were to look forward, toward those who were still waiting to hear.  And he promised that they would receive the Holy Spirit who would strengthen them, guide them, draw them together and send them out.  “If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven them; if you retain the sins of any, they are retained.”  (20:23)


Link to the Second Reading

In the Anchor Bible Dictionary, Pentecost is defined as “the Greek name for the Jewish Feast of Weeks, deriving from its occurrence fifty days after Passover… Thus it was originally an agricultural feast marking the end of the grain harvest and was celebrated during the month of Sivan (May/June).” (Anchor Bible Dictionary, Volume 5, 1992, p. 222.)

This explanation of what the Pentecost festival was for the Jewish people explains why so many Jewish people from so many areas had converged upon Jerusalem as we hear in Acts 2.  In the same way that many of us want to be home for Thanksgiving or Christmas, these people in the first century wanted to be in Jerusalem for the harvest festival Pentecost.

The disciples were no different.  They also wanted to gather with family and loved ones for this harvest festival.  But what the disciples were not expecting, and what the people were shocked to encounter, was that this wasn’t just a holiday of thanksgiving for the harvest or even a chance to reminisce about days gone by.  Instead, God had something new in mind on this Pentecost day.

On this Pentecost, fifty days after Jesus’ resurrection from the dead, God still had more surprises up his sleeve.  As the disciples were gathered together, suddenly a sound like a violent wind occurred and something like tongues of fire rested on each of the disciples. This sound apparently was so massive and so unexpected that a crowd gathered to see what was happening.  As the crowd gathered, the disciples spoke to them.  And when they spoke, everyone gathered could understand them in their own language.

Amazingly, through God’s breath, through the mighty wind of God, the disciples could speak in every language of the world outside their door. Not one language, not two languages, not even sixteen languages, but all heard their own language, from Parthian to Arabic.  Of course, even in the midst of a miraculous encounter, there were skeptics.  But even they had to admit that something was occurring.

People had gathered to remember a holiday.  But God pushed them towards the future.  And after Peter told this confused, elated, amazed group about Jesus, three thousand of the men, women, and children were baptized (3:41).  Something new was blowing in!


Link to the First Reading

The people of God, and apparently the rabble with them (Numbers 11:4), were all complaining about their hardships as they were in the wilderness.  God became angry and sent fire.  But Moses prayed to God and God stopped the fire and even gave them manna and quail to eat.  Still the people complained.  Finally Moses was fed up.  He asked God, “Did I give these people birth?” (11:12)

Moses was overloaded, overwhelmed, and disheartened.  Although he was the leader of the group, the people’s demands had become too much. And so God did a new thing.  God put his Spirit into seventy people so that Moses would have help with the irritable group of people that God had given him!

This new distribution of leadership did not seem to upset Moses at all.  In fact, he welcomed it.  When Joshua complained that two of the men had received the Spirit without being in the right place at the right time, Moses said, “Are you jealous for my sake?  Would that all the LORD’s people were prophets, and that the LORD would put his spirit on them!” (11:30)

Again, God was pushing this group of people towards a new future – a future where things were not always so comfortable.  Even for the people who were used to complaining this brought a whole new set of items to complain about.

I admit that when new things are forced upon me I sometimes rebel.  As people of God, how can we tell when new things are sent by the Spirit of God?  How do we know when “new ideas” are not from God?

If you continued reading this story of the Israelite people in Numbers you would find that even Aaron and Miriam could not always distinguish what was God’s Spirit and what was not.  Perhaps this gives us hope that sometimes it just takes time to see what the Spirit is doing.  In any case, know that on this Pentecost Sunday, the Spirit of God is at work.  It is blowing.  It is free.  And where it ends up only God knows.  But this we know for sure: Always, constantly, something new is blowing in!

Questions for Discussion
  1. The disciples were in the upper room together when Jesus came to them on that first Easter evening.  What do Jesus’ words signify to the disciples?  What are they now called to do and to be?
  2. What is significant about our holiday of Thanksgiving?  What is the primary focus of this day?  How is this day like the Jewish Pentecost?  Could there be something “new” for us to celebrate on Thanksgiving?
  3. The story of Pentecost is pretty much unbelievable for skeptic minds.  What would have been your reaction on that day if you were in the mist of the crowds?  Would you have called the disciples crazy or drunk?  Would you have gone along with the crowd or slipped quietly away? 
  4. How are we a people of history?  A people “of the story”?  How are we a people leaning toward the future?  How do we bring our shared story into the future?
  5. The Israelite people complained against God constantly when they were in the wilderness.  Are there times when our prayers are only complaint?  How does God make our lives new?  With this newness, is there sometimes even more complaint from us?
