Faith

I had the privilege many years ago of meeting with a 10th grade Confirmation student named Julie just before she was to be confirmed.  We went over the Catechism together:  the Lord’s Prayer, the Creed, the Ten Commandments and their meanings. We went over a paper she had written, in which she talked about her faith, what she believed and how she had come to believe it.  Toward the end of our time together, I asked what had been most meaningful for her in Confirmation, what had most struck her in heart, in mind, in soul.

Julie surprised me by bursting out into tears. “Oh,” she said, “that part of the Creed where it talks about how I cannot by my own understanding or effort come to my Lord Jesus or believe in him, but the Holy Spirit has called me through the gospel, enlightened me with his gifts . . . that part.”

When I asked why, the tears grew almost into sobs. Julie was a straight A student, a track star, and a musician budding into great potential. From the moment she got up in the morning to the moment she went to bed at night, her life was committed to work: work as a student, work as an athlete, work as a musician. “Because,” she said, “my faith is the one thing that I don’t have to work at in order to be something. Through Jesus, I just am.”

Today’s texts sound the great theme of the Reformation— we do not earn the gift of salvation through our work; it comes to us freely through our Lord and our Savior Jesus Christ. We are justified by faith through grace. We rely on the promise in the Word only for our standing before God, the word that says it is only faith in Christ alone that saves, through and through by grace alone, not by any hand we’ve had as a part in it.  Through Jesus, I just am, for sure—for now, and for all eternity.

Link to the First Reading

 The prophet writes of a new day, a new time. “The days are surely coming,” says the Lord, “when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah” (Jeremiah 31:31).  The old covenant was no small thing.  It included the promise that Israel would receive the gift of the promised land (Deuteronomy 1:8), and the gift of the Ten Commandments (Deuteronomy 5:6-21). This new covenant would be greater yet. “It will not be like the covenant that I made with their ancestors when I took them by the hand to bring them out of the land of Egypt—a covenant that they broke, though I was their husband, says the LORD” (Jeremiah 31:32).  The old covenant asked for behavior to be changed or shaped externally. The people of God could willfully follow or ignore the law as they saw fit.  There was not necessarily anything internally that led them to follow the law for its own sake, or better, for the sake of the love of God.

This new covenant will go after precisely that which the old could not seem to get at, the heart.  “I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts; and I will be their God, and they shall be my people.”  (Jeremiah 31:33).  The new covenant will put the law in the innermost being of God’s people.  No longer will it be on stone tablets; it will be written in the very fabric their lives. They will follow it as naturally as they will breathe.  And they will not need to remind one another of the Lord their God- everyone will know God, “from the least of them to the greatest” (Jeremiah 31:34). Then the best part of all. With the old covenant,
sin always seemed to have a way of hanging around. Not so with the new.
”I will forgive their iniquity,” God promises, “and remember their sin no more” (Jeremiah 31:34).

Link to the Second Lesson

Here again we hear the call to turn away from idols and to “serve a living and true God.” This letter is written to a young church. They are praised and admonished to continue in the life to which they are called in Christ. The next several weeks take the epistle from Thessalonians. The church is not without opposition and as this letter continues it takes the form of a pastoral letter.

Questions for Discussion:

1. As Christians, we believe that Jesus is the fulfillment of the new covenant. In what ways is this so?  How is the law written in our hearts in Christ?   How do the least to the greatest know God in Christ?  How do we experience the promise of forgiveness in Christ?

2. As long as we live in this world, we still live in the spell of the old covenant, even while we have the promise of life under the new.  In what ways have you experienced life under the old covenant?  In what ways does Christ provide freedom and hope in your journey?

3. A Bible Study was written on this passage called, “New Hearts, New People.” In what ways does that title capture the essence of the text?
4. Why is this text a good text for Reformation Sunday, when we remember and celebrate that we are justified by faith through grace?

Link to the Second Reading

The very first point Paul lays down here is what the law is to be used for. It cannot be used to justify a human being before God. Through the law comes not perfection before God, but “knowledge of sin.” (3:20). The law is like a mirror that reflects who we are, imperfections and all. It is not like a ladder we can climb to find our way into God’s good graces.

The second point Paul makes is that the “righteousness of God” has been revealed “apart from the law” (3:21). The goodness of God isn’t seen in the fulfillment of the law. The goodness of God shows forth “through faith in Jesus Christ for all who believe” (3:22). There is not any distinction between those who are religious and good and those who are not, and bad. “All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God” (3:23); all are “now justified by his grace as a gift” (3:24). It is as it goes in the parable Jesus tells in Matthew 20: the worker who comes along at the end of the day can receive the same wage as the worker who has been at the work all day long. The wage isn’t earned. It’s a surprise, a wonder, a treasure hidden in a field. It comes to the sinner by Christ’s hand, freely.

The third point Paul makes is that justification through faith in Christ Jesus shows, in fact, the glory of God. And the last point he makes is this: There is ground no more for boasting! There is room no more for one to gloat in self-made religiosity, self-sustained morality, the stuff of which self-righteousness is made. It is all “excluded” by the “law of faith” (3:27)—which can allow for no such nonsense. “For we hold that that a person is justified by faith apart from works prescribed by the law” (3:28) Faith in Christ’s love saves; work aimed toward God for the sake of salvation does nothing toward that end.

Questions for Discussion

1. What role does the law play in the life of the believer?  What does faith do in a believer’s life? What happens when faith-work gets mixed up with law-work?  How have you experienced that confusion?  What has resulted for you?

2. Martin Luther has said that “the Christian is the freest person of all, bound to no one; the Christian is the most subject person of all, bound to all.”   How do you understand his words in light of this text?

3. What does salvation by grace through faith mean to you? What do you think it means to the Church in general?  Is it one principle among many?  Or is it the one principle on which the church must stand, even, as the hymn goes, “while steeples are falling”?  Why or why not?

Link to the Gospel

Jesus challenges the Jews who had come to believe in him, saying that if they continue in his word, they would truly be his disciples, and they would know the truth, and the truth would set them free. Those who had come to believe in Jesus took offense at him.  They were descendants of Abraham! They have lived in the freedom of the covenant.  They have never been slaves to anyone. What can Jesus mean, then, in saying that “You will be made free” (8:33)?

Jesus answers with profound truth. Once having committed a sin, you are a slave to it.  The sin inevitably becomes your lord: it holds you in its grip and sway. You end up doing what it says, rather than the other way around. And the slave—the slave does not have a permanent place in the household; that position and place belongs only to the son. So, Jesus argues, if the son makes you free, you will be free indeed (8:36). If the son sets you free from sin, not only are you released from the grip of sin to the ways of the son, you are released from the status of slave to the status of son.  You have a place in the household, a permanent, abiding place. You are free; free indeed.

Questions for Discussion

1.  How have you seen or experienced the truth that once having committed a sin, you are a slave to it?

2. Galatians 5:1 says, “For freedom Christ has set us free.  Stand firm, therefore, and do not submit again to a yoke of slavery.”  What is the freedom for which Christ has set us free? How is it like other freedoms we enjoy in this country?  Unlike them?  What enables us to “stand firm” so that we “do not submit again to a yoke of slavery?”

3.  Why would you want a permanent, abiding place in the household rather than a fleeting one?

4. Why is this a good text for Reformation Sunday?
