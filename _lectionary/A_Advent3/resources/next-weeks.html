Along with being one of my favorite seasons, I find the season of Advent as one of high stress.  We live in a world where the great communicator of the internet is causing us in reality to become more and more isolated.  Increasingly we are surrounded by four walls as studies show we are spending more time at work and less at home.  And when we are home, we are doing more and more things that are work related keeping us in the four walls of our house.  Our connection to the outside world is often relegated to what we read and hear from the media, and we know that is inherently biased with either a conservative or liberal spin. 

Yes, many feel confined and disconnected in a world of uncertainty.  The business of this season, the sad remembrances of the death of loved ones, the loneliness that some people feel can lead to doubt and despair.  If this weren’t enough, we are a world at war.  We are at war with terrorists and with Iraq.  The news and our recent election would suggest that we are at war with one another over what we define as moral issues.  We doubt whether our neighbor sees Jesus in the same way that we do.  Because of this, we too may wonder who Jesus really is in our lives.  During this season of hope, it may sound strange to talk about doubt but clearly doubt and hope have their place this Advent.

Link to the Gospel Reading

Our Gospel comes to bring us hope in this story of a person who wavers between roles.  We can find hope because this story of John the Baptizer reflects our human experience.  Our faith experience is one of journeying back and forth between doubt and certainty, between despair and hope.
 
    As John sits within the four walls of prison he is hearing about the deeds of Christ.  Jesus has done many great things in a very short time, but none of these deeds seem to have helped John.  He is in prison, he will die in prison.  There are no miracles coming his way from the one he called “more powerful.”  John proclaimed the one coming after him to not only be more powerful, but also greater.  John says of the Messiah, I am not worthy to carry his sandals.
 
John may not have been so surprised that he ended up in prison.  Many prophets before him had found themselves in similar circumstances.  But, something was missing for this one who baptized with water.  John believed the Messiah would come and baptize with fire.  Part of that fire likely would have looked like military might.  Yet, as John sits in the walls of prison, doubt comes to him.  Rather than fire, Jesus comes and is baptized with water by John.  Rather than clearing his threshing floor with a winnowing fork, Jesus points to what is seen and heard from him.  “The blind receive their sight, the lame walk, the lepers are cleansed, the deaf hear, the dead are raised, and the poor have good news brought to them.”
 
What do we miss because we expect things to go a certain way?  Do we doubt our children will be successful because their talents are different than ours and we don’t understand them?  Do we doubt our neighbor’s faith because they pull out things from the Gospel that we think are less important, or that miss the point completely?  Do we doubt that we can find happiness in this season because we have lost a loved one, have less money to spend, have stopped believing in Santa Claus and are just too tired to face the season with all its expectation and hype?  Do we doubt Jesus thinking to ourselves, “What has he done for me lately?” 

Jesus didn’t come as John expected him.  John asks of Jesus, “Are you the one who is to come, or are we to wait for another?”  We may ask a similar question of Jesus, “In a day when there are so many voices and signs, which ones point to you Jesus?”  Jesus response to John and his disciples is to tell John what they see Jesus has done.  And then, Jesus talks about what John has done for those who sought him out.  John we are told was more than a prophet.  We are more than what our culture tells us we are.  We are more than our job, our toys, our looks, our role.  We are children of God, inheritors of the Kingdom.

Link to the First Reading

As we have become more and more isolated, I believe Isaiah becomes more and more a relevant book for us.  In a sense we have been exiled from community by the push toward individualism.  I have a friend who blames the loss of community in the Midwest to the invention of the air conditioner.  While before on hot humid days, people would be meeting under the shade of the neighborhood tree, now people go to the comfort of their own homes.  No longer are people drinking lemonade under the Old Oak tree.  We are a dispersed people.

    This passage from Isaiah seeks to give voice to the exiled.  The final line of verse 10 catches the feel for the whole poem.  Sorrow and sighing or another interpretation of the Hebrew, sorrow and groaning shall flee away.  The promise is for everlasting joy and singing shall return.  A word study shows that the same word for sighing or groaning is the word used for woman during childbirth.  I have heard many women claim that they have done more of the latter during childbirth.  The hope is found that the ransomed of the Lord shall return and community will be found again.  The four walls in these verses are put up because of the separation from the community that comes together to worship God.  The hope is found in a savior who with Christian eyes we see is the one who restores sight to the blind, hearing to the deaf, walking to the lame, speech to the mute.

Link to the Second Reading

James is talking to his community that expects that the advent of God’s judgment is at hand.  James expects this judgment to vindicate the poor and to punish the rich.  These words are of comfort for the community that is being persecuted.  The pastoral word is for those in need of strength to last the time of trial.  Here is a call of patience and toward lifting one up in community.  For those who are disgruntled, their anger should be directed toward God who can hear it and not toward one another.

Questions for Discussion



  1. What things in society push you into a mode of isolation and how do you fight against it?

  2. How do you look for the coming of Christ during Advent?

  3. If Jesus were to come back today, what do you think he would be like?

  4. Do you believe there is any relationship between doubt and faith?

  5. Do you find yourself looking forward to Christmas or dreading this season?  How does your faith lead you through this time?
