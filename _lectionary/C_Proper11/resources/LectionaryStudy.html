Focus Statement: “Go and do likewise.” Questions: Seekers of spiritual wisdom have probably existed in every age and every place. The Letter to the Colossians offers a prayer that “you may be filled with the knowledge of God’s will in all spiritual wisdom and understanding, so that you may lead lives worthy of the Lord, fully pleasing to God, as you bear fruit in every good work and as you grow in the knowledge of God.” Some people say that “feeling follows thought,” but one might also say that “doing follows understanding.” What happens when our faith, our trust, our belief translate into action, individually and as a congregation and as a denomination? What does that look like? What are ways that your church expresses its understanding of God’s wisdom in “every good work”? How might that understanding deepen, grow, expand? How does the United Church of Christ expression, “To believe is to care; to care is to do” relate to this question? What does it really look like when we “bear fruit”?What do you think is really in the heart of the lawyer who “stood up to test Jesus”? In his question, is he seeking spiritual wisdom and understanding? What does it mean that he tried to “justify himself”? Is something working on him? Has he made the connection between spiritual wisdom and understanding on the one hand, and his own actions (“every good work”?) on the other? Do you ever identify with him? While the parable of the “good” Samaritan is one of the most familiar stories from the Gospels, it’s the one we mostly get wrong. (If, indeed, one can “get” a parable “right”!) We can’t seem to turn the situation around and understand that, according to Jesus, the one who is helping is “being” the neighbor, not the needy, broken person in the ditch. We think we should help those in need, but isn’t it easy to see ourselves somehow in a superior position to those we help? When we think about our obligations as good and faithful people, isn’t it easy to see ourselves as the one who helps, rather than the one who is being helped? And then, what happens if we receive help from those we despise, or dislike, or disapprove of, or at least avoid? What happens when we put ourselves in the place of the one in the ditch? (What are the ditches in our lives?) Who are the people whose help we would prefer to receive, if indeed we have to accept the help of another? Are their people in our lives who are “Samaritans” in the sense that we see them as “other,” perhaps as enemy, or maybe as someone beyond our narrowly prescribed “neighborhood,” and therefore, certainly not our neighbor? On the other hand, is it sometimes easier to send money to people far away, and not see the people in our own neighborhood, literally or figuratively? Is it easier to send missionaries far away than it is to be in mission in the neighborhood surrounding our own church, or perhaps in the city nearest to us? How does this story told by Jesus challenge us, push us, disturb us? Do you ever feel that it is somehow comforting to express “the law” in such simple, clear terms as loving God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your strength, and with all your mind; and your neighbor as yourself? How might we see “laws” in general as flowing out of these two basic, related commands?

Pew-work Hand-outs

“Pew-work” is like Home-work, except that it is done in the pews, instead of being done at home. Because it is focussed on the readings (as the sermon, presumably, also is) it can be done during the sermon to help the listener concentrate. Or, it can be done while waiting for everyone else to finish their communion. It isn't done during prayers, or hymns, or the readings, because

  • During Prayers, we pray

  • During Hymns, we sing

During the Readings, we listen
