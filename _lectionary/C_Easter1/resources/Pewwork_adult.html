Focus Statement: “Why do you look for the living among the dead?”

  1. Just when all hope seemed dead and gone, the followers of Jesus experience something incredible – the resurrection of Jesus! This is truly God doing “a new thing,” and “the former things shall not be remembered or come to mind” (from the Isaiah reading). When we are sifting through the dead places of our lives, there may be signs of new life that we are missing. How is this true in the life of our congregation, and in our own lives as individuals, or as people in relationship?

  1. The road to Jerusalem, and the time Jesus has spent there, has been difficult. And yet the words, “Jerusalem as a joy,” have power in our imaginations, when we think of the city Jesus loved so well as a place transformed by God’s hand. Think then of the places in our lives – cities, towns, suburbs, rural areas – as places of salvation, healing, renewal, resurrection. How have we felt the hand of God at work in every effort to bring justice and mercy to those in need, to those at the edges of our communities?

Isaiah describes a different way of ordering human life, in whatever setting: the cry of distress will not be heard, our babies will not die in infancy, and even folks one hundred years old will be considered young. The people will live in the houses they build and live on the harvest of their own labors, rather than watching others profit from their work. “They shall not hurt or destroy on all my holy mountain, says the Lord.” What is the meaning for us today in these words, if we are struggling to build communities of hope?
