Bible Passages

2 Samuel 18:5-9, 15, 31-33 and Psalm 130


or 1 Kings 19:4-8 and Psalm 34:1-8,

Today's reading carries on the theme of God providing for the people in unexpected ways during a wilderness experience. This story is about Elijah's journey during the great drought that afflicted the reign of King Ahab. (Children's chat will present another vignette from that time). It is interesting to consider to what degree the drought was a punishment, and to what degree it was a natural event that was distinguished by God's choice of whom God sustained and supported. Droughts and natural disasters affect the poor and just (like the widow of Zarapath) as well as the powerful and corrupt (like Ahab). How people are affected by the disaster is the critical determiner of whether they will see it as punishment (as did Ahab) or as a “wilderness experience” distinguished by relying on God's grace.

Ephesians 4:25-5:2,

More on the challenge of building up the community, distinguishing between encouraging one another in righteousness with discernment and wisdom, and the judgementalism that tears down community. Anger in itself is not a sin “Be Angry” is in the imperative voice – it is a command! But “Do not sin” is also imperative. One can experience and acknowledge emotions, without allowing them to take control and override our love and commitment to one another.

John 6:35, 41-51

Last week's reading culminated in Jesus' assertion that He is “the Bread of Life”. This week's reading starts with that assertion and draws out the implications of what that means to us who have faith in Him.

The promise “No one can come to me unless drawn by the Father who sent me” must surely cause us pause when we are tempted to squabble or judge within the church, and divide the body. Everyone who comes to Christ was specifically drawn to Christ by the Father. Rejecting or setting up barriers places us in direct opposition to God's will.

Pew-work Hand-outs

“Pew-work” is like Home-work, except that it is done in the pews, instead of being done at home. Because it is focussed on the readings (as the sermon, presumably, also is) it can be done during the sermon to help the listener concentrate. Or, it can be done while waiting for everyone else to finish their communion. It isn't done during prayers, or hymns, or the readings, because

  • During Prayers, we pray

  • During Hymns, we sing

During the Readings, we listen
