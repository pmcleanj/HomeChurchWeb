Fifth Sunday of Easter

Revelation 22:12-14, 16-17, 20-21

POST-RESURRECTION BLUES

It is the Seventh Sunday of Easter and I am already tired. Maybe if the Easter season were not so long I could maintain the enthusiasm. I could continue to shout with unwavering conviction, “He is risen indeed! Alleluia!” But when I look around my life, what is there really to shout about? I have dishes piled high in the sink, work is as challenging as ever with its above average demands and below average access to resources, and my husband and I just received a dental bill the size of a small retirement fund. Life is the same routine monotonous boredom it has always been.

I sometimes think that the season of Easter can be even more depressing than the season of Lent. At least when we are still waiting for the resurrection, we can still have some hope, some anticipation of what is to come. But what happens when you come out of the other side of the cross and nothing has changed? Everything remains the same. As the resurrection fades to a distant memory, the stagnant waters of our renewed baptism become murkier and murkier. Was this what the world was waiting for?

Link to the First Reading

Each generation of Christians has had to struggle with how to live in a post-resurrection world, beginning with Jesus’ own generation. Jesus’ beloved disciples had the advantage of living in both a pre-resurrection and a post-resurrection world – before the cross and after the cross. Moreover, they had instructions from Jesus’ own mouth of how to continue after he was gone. Having been commissioned to go out and spread the good news and lay down the foundations for the community of faith, it was essential to their mission that they continued to believe that the good news was indeed good news.

As the disciples soon discovered, the post-resurrection world was not a world void of violence and suffering. In Acts 16:16-34, Paul and Silas are arrested at Philippi for doing the work of God and casting out evil spirits. They are tortured and chastised and thrown into prison (22-24). Despite their suffering, they continue to pray and sing hymns to God (25), tremendous acts of faith in the midst of such dire circumstances. We quickly learn that though the resurrection has not yet eradicated the world of such evil forces, they are not enough to thwart the will and the work of God. For through divine intervention, an earthquake shatters the prison walls and breaks Paul and Silas free from their bonds (26). And on top of that, it is through this unfortunate ordeal that another unbeliever comes to faith (33)! Though God may not have desired the suffering of the disciples to take place in the first place, God is able to turn it around and make something positive come out of it.

Link to the Second Reading

This is good news indeed, but is this all there is? Did Christ come and suffer and die and be raised again so that we could all come and suffer and die and be raised to? From the earliest strands of our Jewish history, long before Jesus of Nazareth had taken flesh, our Messianic expectations have hoped for something more, a new age, a new Jerusalem where suffering and dying no longer exist. The book of Revelation is the place in the New Testament where such hopes are most strongly flushed out.

Revelation has not only been one of the most controversial books of the New Testament in the history of Christian scholarship but it has also been one of the most abused books. Many have treated the book as a coded analysis for unlocking current events and even predicting the date of the end of the world. Such attempts, while they may be grounded in genuine seeking, are unfaithful to the genre itself. Apocalyptic writings are by nature rich in mystery and vivid symbology that relates present events to the hope of a future reality. Thus it is not as likely that the author of revelation would have been prophesying about future events such as the Holocaust or 9/11 as he or she would have been more apt to relate to historical atrocities from its own time period such as the very real threat of persecution under the Roman Emperors Nero and Domitian.

Whatever our relationship with this mysterious book has been in the past, we should not allow our fear and confusion to prevent us from approaching the book and gaining its wonderful theological insights. For aside from its intimidating imagery and impending prophecies of doom, when read in its entirety the book of revelation contains some of the earliest most powerful Christian liturgy as well as an inspiring vision for the redemption of all of creation. Jesus is coming again, and no matter how literal, how gradual, how cosmic you understand that to be, what you are left with is a world where the bridge between heaven and earth has been gapped and a moving invitation for everyone to be a part of it:

“The Spirit and the bride say, “come.”
And let everyone who hears say, “come.”
And let everyone who is thirsty come. (Rev 22:17)

In the Epilogue of this last book of the Bible, we are reminded that Jesus is coming soon (12, 20). And in faith we realize that our deepest liturgical response to the Call to Worship “He is risen,” is not simply “He is risen indeed,” but “Amen. Come, Lord Jesus!”

Link to the Gospel

As Christians in this generation we still live between the tension of “He is risen indeed!” and “Amen. Come, Lord Jesus!” It is a difficult place to live, between the already and the not yet, and it can only be lived in faith. But the good news is that it is not a place that we dwell alone. For we are always in the company of other people of faith as well.

In the night before Jesus’ betrayal, Jesus offers up a prayer (John 17:20-26). His prayer is that we may be one, just as Jesus is one with the Father (21). Jesus’ prayer gives us an image of Christian community based on the unity of the Father and the Son. Just as God dwells in Jesus, Jesus is present in the community of faith. And in making God’s love known to us, we are able to love one another and be one in Christ (26).

We may not know the exact way and the exact time and the exact place in which God’s salvific history will work itself out, but in Christian community we are not alone. Not only are we able to love one another but in that love Christ is present. The lines between the already and the not yet become blurred and the liturgy becomes reality. The Easter promise has not faded. Christ is coming soon, and yet, if you look around he is already here, present in the community of faith.

QUESTIONS FOR DISCUSSION

  1. What events in your life or images in the media make it difficult to believe that the resurrection has had any impact?

  2. In Acts, Paul and Silas go through a terrible ordeal in being arrested and beaten, yet God is able to make something good come out of it. Tell about a time when you have witnessed or gone through something negative and God has been able to make something positive out of it.

  3. Many Christians have had a difficult time with the book of Revelation, not really knowing what to do with it. Even Martin Luther himself treated it as secondary Scripture and practically dispensable. What has your relationship been like with the book of revelation? Jot down as many interpretations as you can think of that you have heard other people say about Revelation. Take some time to sit down and read the book in one sitting? What themes and images do you notice? What in the book do you react negatively to? What do you react positively to? When read as a whole do you think it is appropriate to isolate certain passages and try and interpret them apart from the rest of the book in correlation with current events unfolding in history?

  4. As Christians in a post-resurrection society, we live in between the already resurrected Christ and the not yet fully redeemed creation. In what ways do you feel this tension in your own life or community?

  5. Jesus’ final prayer for us was that we might be one as he and the Father were one. When you look at the community of faith, do you think we are one yet? If not where are we going wrong? What do we need to do to become united as Jesus prayed for us?
